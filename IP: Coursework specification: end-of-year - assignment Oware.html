Scenario: The Game of Oware

Adapted from wikipedia under Creative Commons Attribution ShareAlike.

The only substantive difference is in 'ending the game'. Oware is an abstract strategy game among the bigger Mancala family of board games (or Pit and Pebbles Games) played all over the world with slight variations across different societies with regard to distribution area, number of players and strategy of play.

Rules (abapa variation)

The game requires an oware board and 48 seeds. An oware board has two straight rows of six pits, called "houses", and one large "score" house at either end. Each player controls the six houses on their side of the board, and the score house on their end. The game begins with four seeds in each of the twelve smaller houses.
The object of the game is to capture more seeds than one's opponent. Since the game has only 48 seeds, capturing 25 is sufficient to accomplish this. Since there is an even number of seeds, it is possible for the game to end in a draw, where each player has captured 24.

Sowing

Players take turns moving the seeds. On a turn, a player chooses one of the six houses under their control. The player removes all seeds from that house, and distributes them, dropping one in each house counter-clockwise from this house, in a process called sowing. Seeds are not distributed into the end scoring houses, nor into the house drawn from. That is, the starting house is left empty; if it contained 12 seeds, it is skipped, and the twelfth seed is placed in the next house. The diagram shows the result of sowing from house E.

Capturing

After a turn, seeds may be captured from every consecutive house on the opponent's side whose seed total was brought to two or three on the player's current turn. They are placed in the player's scoring house. If the last seed placed into an opponent's house brought the house's total to exactly two or three, all the seeds in that house are captured. If the previous-to-last seed also brought an opponent's house to two or three, these are captured as well, and so on until a house is reached which does not contain two or three seeds. However, if a move would capture all of an opponent's seeds, the capture is forfeited since this would prevent the opponent from continuing the game, and the seeds are instead left on the board. In the diagram the lower player would capture all the seeds in houses e, d, and c but not b (as it has four seeds) or a (since it is not contiguous to the other captured houses).

Let the opponent play

The proscription against capturing all an opponent's seeds is related to a more general idea, that one ought to make a move that allows the opponent to continue playing. If an opponent's houses are all empty, the current player must make a move that gives the opponent seeds. If no such move is possible, the current player captures all seeds in his/her own territory, ending the game.

Ending the game

The game is over when one player has captured 25 or more seeds, or each player has taken 24 seeds (draw). If a cycle is made where a board state is repeated (regardless of which player's turn it is) then each player captures all the seeds on their side of the board and the game is over. If 100 consecutive moves are made without any seeds being captured by either player then the game is ended: the result is decided by the number of seeds in the score houses at that point.

Problem

A set of Java interfaces is provided: do not change any of the interfaces as your work will be compiled against the originals. You can also view the javadoc for the interfaces. Static methods and constructors are specified in the class description where appropriate.

(40%) Write a two-player Oware game in Java with a textual user interface. You should write it for two human players and assume that the players will take turns. Do this by writing classes implementing each of the interfaces specified apart from GameManager. Provide a single implementation of the Player interface which works by asking the user (via the console) where to play. This game could be played through bluej by repeatedly calling the nextMove() method and checking getResult().
